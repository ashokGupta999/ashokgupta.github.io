Research

My research sits at the intersection of aerosols, clouds, climate, and land-surface processes — using observations, modeling, machine learning, and inverse techniques to address aerosol–cloud–climate challenges.

Research Interests

1

Convection & Mixed-Phase Cloud Processes

Investigating how aerosols modulate mixed-phase and deep convective clouds — altering precipitation efficiency and storm dynamics — and probing which microphysical pathways dominate ice amplification in midlatitude convective anvils (Waman et al., 2022; Gupta et al., 2023).

2

Land–Atmosphere Feedbacks & Aerosol Emissions

Examining how land-use change, surface-wind variability, soil-moisture anomalies, and wildfire combine to control dust aerosol loading; assessing how these coupled forcings drive extremes from regional heatwaves to progressive land degradation.

3

Remote Sensing Retrievals of Aerosol & Cloud Properties

Developing satellite- and ground-based algorithms to constrain key aerosol metrics (AOD, size distribution, single-scattering albedo) and cloud properties (liquid & ice water paths, effective radius) — essential for narrowing uncertainties in Earth's radiation budget.

4

Volcanic Influences on Stratospheric Composition & Radiation

Through detailed case studies — most recently the 2022 Hunga eruption — quantifying how volcanic injections of water vapor and SO₂ perturb stratospheric chemistry and radiative forcing, and evaluating their impacts on global surface temperatures (Gupta et al., 2022, 2025).

5

Real-Time Automated Detection of Volcanic Umbrella Clouds

Using machine learning (patch-wise ConvLSTM auto-encoder), retrieving volcanic umbrella-cloud tops at sub-km resolution from a single visible/IR frame, cutting latency from hours to seconds. The system tracked 98% of umbrella-top evolution during the April 2024 Ruang eruption (Gupta & Bennartz, manuscript ready).

Current Research

As Staff Scientist at Vanderbilt University, I am developing deep-learning–enhanced satellite algorithms for retrieving ice-cloud microphysical properties in support of NASA's PolSIR (Polarized Submillimeter Ice-cloud Radiometer) mission, delivering global, all-weather measurements of ice clouds to constrain their impact on Earth's radiation budget.

I have reconstructed the 21st-century global dust cycle by combining ground-based and satellite observations, three aerosol reanalyses, and a large-ensemble inverse modeling framework. We show that global dust aerosol optical depth declined by −10 ± 8 % between 2003 and 2023 — the strongest evidence to date that the planet has become less dusty in the early 21st century. This work is now accepted in Science Advances (2026).

In parallel, I contributed to an observationally constrained framework quantifying the longwave direct radiative forcing of desert dust. We constrain the present-day clear-sky dust longwave DRE to +0.25 ± 0.06 W m⁻² — nearly double current climate model estimates. This work is now accepted in Nature Communications (2026).

I also continue developing a physics-informed, scene-adaptive machine-learning model that detects volcanic umbrella clouds in near-real time using Himawari-8/9 imagery, and I am investigating with deep-learning–based PCA the drivers of the 21st-century decline in dust aerosols.

Research Interests

My research sits at the intersection of aerosols, clouds, climate, and land‑surface processes.

1. Convection and mixed‑phase cloud processes:

Focusing on secondary ice production, I investigate how aerosols modulate mixed‑phase and deep convective clouds—altering precipitation efficiency and storm dynamics—and probe which microphysical pathways dominate ice amplification in midlatitude convective anvils (Waman et al., 2022; Gupta et al., 2023).

2. Land–Atmosphere Feedbacks and Aerosol Emissions:

I examine how land‑use change, surface‑wind variability, soil‑moisture anomalies, and wildfire combine to control dust aerosol loading and assess how these coupled forcings drive extremes, from regional heatwaves and severe air‑quality crises to progressive land degradation and desertification.

3. Remote sensing retrievals of aerosol and cloud properties:

I develop and apply satellite‑ and ground‑based algorithms to constrain key aerosol metrics (AOD, size distribution, asymmetry parameters, single‑scattering albedo) and cloud properties (liquid & ice water paths, droplet effective radius). These retrievals are essential for narrowing uncertainties in Earth’s radiation budget and validating climate models.

4. Volcanic influences on stratospheric composition and radiation:

Through detailed case studies—most recently the 2022 Hunga eruption—I quantify how volcanic injections of water vapor and SO₂ perturb stratospheric chemistry and radiative forcing, and evaluate their impacts on global surface temperatures (Gupta et al., 2022, 2025).

5. Real‑Time automated detection of volcanic umbrella clouds from Geostationary observations:

I employ lightweight deep‑learning pipelines to transform raw satellite radiances into science‑grade information within minutes. Using a patch‑wise ConvLSTM auto‑encoder, I retrieve volcanic umbrella‑cloud tops at sub‑kilometre resolution from a single high‑resolution visible or IR frame, cutting latency from hours to seconds while keeping thermal errors below 2 K. When paired with a physically based cold‑anomaly mask, the system tracked 98 % of umbrella‑top evolution during the April 2024 Ruang eruption, demonstrating its clear value for rapid ash‑hazard warnings and first‑order radiative‑forcing estimates.

Current Research

I’m currently reconstructing the 21st‑century global dust cycle by combining ground‑based and satellite observations, reanalysis data, and inverse modeling techniques (Gupta et al., in prep), aiming to quantify dust variability and its radiative impact across major source regions. This work provides critical constraints on dust–climate feedbacks in Earth system models. I am also investigating how land‑use changes, vegetation dynamics, and soil moisture influence dust aerosol emissions and associated shifts in Earth’s energy balance.

News Coverage

Our recent study, which revealed unexpected Southern Hemisphere cooling following the 2022 Hunga Tonga eruption, was featured in major scientific outlets such as UCLA News. These findings challenge the conventional view of post‑eruption warming and underscore the critical role of sulfate aerosol radiative effects.

Another study on Hunga eruption used satellite remote sensing to characterize the umbrella clouds generated by the 19 December 2021, 13 January 2022, and 15 January 2022 eruptions of Hunga Tonga–Hunga Ha’apai. We identified two distinct cloud layers on 15 January (upper at 31 ± 3 km, lower at 17 ± 2 km), measured a peak volumetric flow rate of ~5.0 × 10¹¹ m³ s⁻¹—nearly two orders of magnitude higher than earlier events—and confirmed that all umbrella clouds were ice‑rich.

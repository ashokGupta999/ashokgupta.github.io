Research

Aerosols, clouds, climate, and land-surface processes — using observations, modeling, machine learning, and inverse techniques.

Research Interests

1. Convection & Mixed-Phase Cloud Processes

Investigating how aerosols modulate mixed-phase and deep convective clouds — altering precipitation efficiency and storm dynamics — and probing which microphysical pathways dominate ice amplification in midlatitude convective anvils (Waman et al., 2022; Gupta et al., 2023).

2. Arctic Mixed-Phase Clouds, INP Parameterization & Secondary Ice Production

Studying how mineral-dust and biological ice-nucleating particles (INPs) and secondary ice production (SIP) mechanisms — rime splintering, ice–ice collisional breakup, freezing-drop fragmentation, and sublimation breakup — govern the liquid–ice partitioning of high-latitude mixed-phase clouds and their influence on the polar surface energy budget and sea-ice projections.

3. Polar Cloud Radiative Effects & NASA PREFIRE

Leveraging NASA's PREFIRE calibrated far-infrared/infrared spectral observations (~5–54 µm) to characterize spectrally resolved longwave cloud radiative effects across polar regions, enabling regime-dependent diagnostics and evaluation of reanalyses and climate models directly in measurement space.

4. Land–Atmosphere Feedbacks & Aerosol Emissions

Examining how land-use change, surface-wind variability, soil-moisture anomalies, and wildfire combine to control dust aerosol loading; assessing how these coupled forcings drive extremes from regional heatwaves to progressive land degradation.

5. Remote Sensing Retrievals of Aerosol & Cloud Properties

Developing satellite- and ground-based algorithms to constrain key aerosol metrics (AOD, size distribution, single-scattering albedo) and cloud properties (liquid & ice water paths, effective radius) — essential for narrowing uncertainties in Earth's radiation budget.

6. Volcanic Influences on Stratospheric Composition & Radiation

Through detailed case studies — most recently the 2022 Hunga eruption — quantifying how volcanic injections of water vapor and SO₂ perturb stratospheric chemistry and radiative forcing, and evaluating their impacts on global surface temperatures (Gupta et al., 2022, 2025).

7. Real-Time Automated Detection of Volcanic Umbrella Clouds

Using machine learning (patch-wise ConvLSTM auto-encoder), retrieving volcanic umbrella-cloud tops at sub-km resolution from a single visible/IR frame, cutting latency from hours to seconds. The system tracked 98% of umbrella-top evolution during the April 2024 Ruang eruption (Gupta & Bennartz, manuscript ready).

Current Research

As Staff Scientist at Vanderbilt University, I am developing deep-learning–enhanced satellite algorithms for retrieving ice-cloud microphysical properties in support of NASA's PolSIR (Polarized Submillimeter Ice-cloud Radiometer) mission, delivering global, all-weather measurements of ice clouds to constrain their impact on Earth's radiation budget.

I have reconstructed the 21st-century global dust cycle by combining ground-based and satellite observations, three aerosol reanalyses, and a large-ensemble inverse modeling framework. We show that global dust aerosol optical depth declined by −10 ± 8 % between 2003 and 2023 — the strongest evidence to date that the planet has become less dusty in the early 21st century. This work is now accepted with minor revision in Science Advances (2026).

In parallel, I contributed to an observationally constrained framework quantifying the longwave direct radiative forcing of desert dust. We constrain the present-day clear-sky dust longwave DRE to +0.25 ± 0.06 W m⁻² — nearly double current climate model estimates. This work is now accepted in Nature Communications (2026).

I also continue developing a physics-informed, scene-adaptive machine-learning model that detects volcanic umbrella clouds in near-real time using Himawari-8/9 imagery, and I am investigating with deep-learning–based PCA the drivers of the 21st-century decline in dust aerosols.

A new line of research uses NASA's PREFIRE calibrated far-infrared and infrared spectral radiances (~5–54 µm) to quantify spectrally resolved longwave cloud radiative effects across both polar regions. By diagnosing cloud–thermodynamic interactions in spectral measurement space rather than broadband totals, this work aims to identify regime-dependent radiative fingerprints and provide observationally constrained benchmarks for evaluating reanalyses and climate models.

I am also investigating the impact of mineral-dust ice-nucleating particles (INPs) on Arctic mixed-phase clouds and secondary ice production (SIP). This work examines how composition-aware INP parameterizations and multiple SIP mechanisms — including rime splintering, ice–ice collisional breakup, freezing-drop fragmentation, and sublimation breakup — interact to control the liquid–ice partitioning that governs the high-latitude surface energy budget, with implications for sea-ice and permafrost projections.

Featured Publications

The January 2022 Hunga eruption cooled the Southern Hemisphere in 2022 and 2023

Gupta et al., 2025 · Nature Portfolio Journal: Communications Earth & Environment

Quantifying the climatic effects of the 2022 Hunga Tonga volcanic eruption, this research revealed a significant net radiative cooling effect (−0.55 ± 0.05 W m⁻² in 2022, −0.26 ± 0.04 W m⁻² in 2023) due to sulfate aerosols, causing a temperature drop of about −0.10 ± 0.02 K in the Southern Hemisphere. This unexpected cooling contradicts initial predictions of volcanic‑induced warming, underlining complex volcanic impacts on climate.

Bottom line: Hunga eruption produced measurable cooling (~−0.10 K), challenging initial climate projections. From Gupta et al. (2025).

Warm vs. Cold Rain in Storms

Gupta et al., 2023 · Nature Portfolio Journal: Communications Earth & Environment

Simulating continental storms with cloud‑base temperatures ranging from cold (below 0 °C) to very warm (~20 °C), researchers found warm‑rain processes accounted for up to ~80 % of surface precipitation in very warm–based clouds. Conversely, cold‑based clouds produced predominantly ice‑crystal‑driven precipitation, highlighting strong dependency on cloud‑base temperatures.

Bottom line: Cloud‑base warming (~10–20 K) shifts precipitation formation dominantly towards warm‑rain processes (~80 %). From Gupta et al. (2023).

Volcanic Umbrella Clouds: Insights from Hunga Tonga

Gupta et al., 2022 · Nature Portfolio Journal: Communications Earth & Environment

Satellite observations of the Hunga Tonga eruption detected unprecedented volcanic umbrella cloud heights (~31 km) and rapid volumetric expansion rates, forming two distinct ice‑rich umbrella layers. These remarkable features significantly inform models of eruption dynamics, global climate impact, and aviation safety.

Bottom line: Record‑high volcanic clouds (~31 km) redefine understanding of eruption impacts and global climate. From Gupta et al. (2022).

Observational Evidence of Enhanced Daytime Tropical Deep Convective Clouds

Gupta et al., 2018 · Atmospheric Research

Investigating CloudSat and CALIPSO data, the study reported a pronounced daytime increase (~10–15 % enhancement) in thick tropical high‑altitude clouds (>12 km). This contrasts nighttime maxima in mid‑level clouds (5–12 km) and results from intense afternoon convection and persistent cirrus clouds, significantly influencing tropical radiative heating rates and Earth’s radiation budget.

Bottom line: Daytime cloud enhancement above 12 km altitude (~10–15 %) significantly impacts tropical weather and climate systems. From Gupta et al. (2018).

Diurnal Cycles of Cloud Radiative Forcing

Gupta et al., 2020 · Climate Dynamics

This research, using Megha‑Tropiques satellite data, quantified large diurnal variations in tropical cloud radiative forcing. Nighttime longwave warming (−220 to −300 W m⁻²) peaks in the early afternoon, with an amplitude of ~15–25 W m⁻² (20–35 %), crucial for climate modeling accuracy.

Bottom line: Diurnal cloud forcing variations (up to 25 W m⁻²) significantly modulate Earth’s tropical energy balance. From Gupta et al. (2021).

Marine Stratocumulus Clouds and Their Radiative Effect

Gupta et al., 2017 · Journal of Atmospheric & Solar‑Terrestrial Physics

Multi‑year CloudSat & CALIPSO analysis (2006–2010) over subtropical oceans shows nighttime thickening of marine stratocumulus (~960–1200 m) boosts long‑wave warming by 10–20 W m⁻², highlighting critical day–night variations influencing Earth’s energy balance.

Bottom line: Night‑time thickening enhances stratocumulus warming effect (~10–20 W m⁻²), vital for precise climate modeling. From Gupta et al. (2017).

Ice Multiplication in Convective Storms (Co-authored Collaborative Publication)

Waman et al., 2022 · Journal of Atmospheric Sciences

Analyzing four secondary ice production mechanisms in numerical simulations validated with airborne observations, this study found ice multiplication was highest (enhancement ratio ~10³) near −12 °C. Fragmentation in ice–ice collisions and the Hallett–Mossop process dominate ice enhancement, significantly influencing cloud‑precipitation dynamics.

Bottom line: Ice multiplication peaks (~10³ enhancement) near −12 °C, critical for accurate precipitation forecasting.

Biological Aerosols and Ice Formation in Clouds (Co-authored Collaborative Publication)

Patade et al., 2022 · Atmospheric Chemistry & Physics

Utilizing a detailed aerosol–cloud model simulation, this study demonstrated biological aerosols (fungi, bacteria, pollen) have limited impact on cloud ice formation. Even increasing biological particle loadings by factors of 100–1000 led to less than ~6 % changes in ice number concentrations and minimal effects (< 4 %) on precipitation and radiative fluxes, due to dominant secondary ice production mechanisms.

Bottom line: Biological aerosols minimally affect cloud microphysics (< 6 %) and precipitation (< 4 %), refining cloud–aerosol interaction models.
